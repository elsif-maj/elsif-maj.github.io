I have no illusions that web application development will be my primary skillset at the tail end of this journey, and key to my eventual employment as a developer. I also, in heeding the advice of other LS grads, know that deviating too much from the curriculum is a mistake given its substantial and dense content, and the dangers of going down rabbitholes in a field of study that is as wide and deep as computing. With these thoughts as cautions, I have excitedly acquired a few highly regarded books to satiate my curiosities regarding some of the more basic computing and computer science topics that pique my interest which I think will complement my LS studies.

The first is the evergreen `The C Programming Language` -- affectionately referred to often in writing with the 'K&R' shorthand in reference to authors and programming icons Brian Kernighan and Dennis Ritchie. I dipped into the first chapter of this book and greatly enjoyed it while studying basic Ruby concepts for an assessment. However, now that I am on to learning new material and trying to absorb everything related to object oriented programming in Ruby, I have put this book down for now. I'm excited to return to it to poke around when I am not actively learning a lot of new syntax for the LS curriculum, which may not be very often.

The second is Charles Petzold's `Code`, which will be the only book of my trio that I will actually end up reading in full anytime soon. In `Code`, Petzold attempts to take the reader on a tour of computer systems that builds from absolute basics and first principles, and takes one all of the way to relatively technical descriptions of memory, CPUs, OSs, etc. Uniquely, Petzold presumes no prior exposure to most of the principles and ideas necessary to understand computer systems, but instead starts with engagine descriptions of basic number theory, code, and electrical circuits, and builds up layers of complexity and abstraction from there.

Lastly, there is the modern classic and absolute dumptruck of a tome that is `Computer Systems: A Programmer's Perspective`. This is in many ways, the level at which I could hope to casually end up if I can steal enough moments over the course of the LS curriculum to learn C along the way. This in depth exploration of computer systems assumes familiarity with C and programming fluency, and involves an interactive exploration of computer subsystems and low level software via code in addition to a large amount of technical exposition. This is a bit of a... finishing school that I hope I can be in a position to *start* by the time I am applying for jobs in the field that have a very different emphasis.

It will be interesting to see if I manage to find the time for this bit of computer science injection into my studies.

I'm Still Studying (2-12-22)

I passed the first part of the first Launchschool exam a few weeks back, and I have since been studying a little bit each day for the second half of the exam. These first exams -- covering nothing less than 'Programming Foundations' -- test ability over a wide possible range of contents.

Because I came to the craft of programming without having any background (just a few months ago,) and because these initial tests are so substantial, I have found myself having to answer a call to patience for the present time. Each day I practice the craft a bit and I allow time and consistent effort to calcify my new abilities.

The practice of writing code is itself inherently satisfying, and so is the confidence gained from knowing that I will not move forward to new concepts until I have mastered the ones at hand. I feel liberated from having to design my study towards clearing a bar by a deadline and can instead focus on genuinely transforming my capacities.
